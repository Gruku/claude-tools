## Preamble

Typography gives the system its voice. Color gives it atmosphere. Spacing & Grid gives it **structure** — the spatial logic that determines where everything lives, how elements relate to each other, and how the user moves through what the system presents.

Both preceding primitives have already been making spatial promises they cannot fully keep on their own. Typography established territorial whitespace for the Declaration, comfortable line height for the Narrator, and the dense packing of the Technical voice. Color established value-based elevation and surface separation. These are spatial commitments living as guests in other primitives' documents. Spacing & Grid gives them a proper home — and adds the structural scaffold that organizes how type and color are deployed together.

This document defines two fundamentals — **Framing** and **Composition** — the spatial forces that govern every layout decision in Reality Reprojection. Framing describes the relationship between content and its container. Composition describes the relationships between elements within that container. Together, they produce the spatial identity of the system across every medium — from a poster to an OLED to an AR environment.

Specific grid systems, spacing scales, breakpoint rules, and responsive behaviors are Layer 3 concerns. What Layer 2 establishes is what the system *believes* about space and how those beliefs constrain the decisions Layer 3 will make.

---

## A Cross-Spatial Trait — Space is Engineered

Across both fundamentals, Reality Reprojection's space is **authored with intent**. Every distance, every margin, every gap between elements is a deliberate decision — not a default, not a leftover, not a side effect of content flow.

This is the spatial equivalent of Typography's "Presence over Delicacy" and Color's concentric independence model. It is the instinct that governs every spatial choice before specific rules apply.

But *engineered* does not mean *rigid*. Spacing & Grid is designed with the understanding that the structure will be **inhabited** — by users who zoom to 150%, who hide elements, who open panels side by side, who use the system on screens and in contexts the designer did not anticipate. Like a well-made physical product, the spatial structure has an intended form, but it is built to survive contact with the real conditions of use. A chair is engineered for sitting, but people lean back, cross their legs, perch on the armrest. The chair holds.

This dual nature — authored precision that accommodates inhabited variation — connects directly to Layer 1's "Constraints generate clarity" and "Design is alive until it stops changing." The constraints are the engineering. The life is the inhabitation.

Space is engineered also means that **empty space is active, not absent.** A margin is not "nothing" — it is a spatial decision that communicates containment, breathing room, importance, or separation. Whitespace in this system has the same status as a typographic voice or a color role: it is doing a job. If it is not doing a job, it should not be there. If it *is* there, it was placed with purpose.
