## Preamble

Typography gives the system its voice. Color gives it atmosphere. Spacing gives it structure. Motion gives it time. Material Vocabulary gives it **substance** — the optical quality that makes elements feel like they are made of something, not drawn onto nothing.

Every preceding primitive has been making material promises it cannot keep on its own. Color established value-based elevation — surfaces at different depths — but how those surfaces *look* at their boundaries is unmapped. Spacing established depth layers as nested fields — but the visual expression of those layers (shadow, blur, edge treatment, translucency) belongs here.

This document gives those promises a proper home. It defines the **material properties of the field** — the optical characteristics that determine how surfaces exist, how boundaries behave, how depth becomes visible, and how the system achieves physical presence without physical simulation.

Material Vocabulary is not a library of effects. It is the answer to a question the other primitives keep asking: *what are these things made of?*

---

## A Cross-Material Trait — Manufactured, Not Grown

Across every material decision in Reality Reprojection, the governing instinct is that surfaces feel **manufactured** — precision-made, considered, intentional. Not organic, not accidental, not simulated from nature.

This is the material expression of a preference that runs through the entire system: LEGO bricks, machined aluminum, Parker pens, clockwork mechanisms. Things that were *made with tooling*. Clean edges. Consistent surfaces. The satisfaction of a well-manufactured object where every surface was a decision, not a happenstance.

Manufactured does not mean cold or sterile. A LEGO brick is manufactured and playful. Machined aluminum is manufactured and warm to the touch. The quality is *precision with care* — the same dual nature that Motion's Clockwork describes: function that is simultaneously craft.

This also means the system does not simulate natural materials. No wood grain, no leather texture, no paper curl. When the system references physicality — and it does, constantly — it references the physicality of *designed objects*: the matte finish of an injection-molded surface, the frosted quality of etched glass, the clean radius of a CNC-milled edge. The materials are real. They are just not *natural*.

## Key Material Properties

| Property | Purpose | Character |
|----------|---------|-----------|
| **Opacity** | Control visibility of what's behind | Default: slightly translucent |
| **Blur** | Distort what shows through | Frosted (not transparent) |
| **Surface Finish** | Tactile quality | Default: quality matte |
| **Edge Treatment** | Boundaries | Clean, considered, precise |
| **Depth Expression** | Layer visibility | Combination of above |
