## The Narrator

*The voice that carries you through. The one you spend the most time with. The one that should never make you aware of itself.*

### The Job

The Narrator handles sustained reading. Blog posts, documentation, descriptions, explanations, long-form content. Its primary job is *comfort* — not in the sense of softness, but in the sense of never creating friction between the reader and the content. You should be able to read the Narrator for twenty minutes and not once think about the typeface. It also handles structural roles: section headers, pull quotes, interface labels. The Narrator is the everyday voice.

### The Character

Geometrically simple. Sans-serif. Humanist in its curves — not a grid-snapped geometric sans, but not a calligraphic humanist either. Somewhere in the middle where the shapes are clean but the strokes carry just enough organic variation to feel made by hands, not plotted by coordinates.

Calm. That is the defining quality. Not inert — calm. The typographic equivalent of a clear, unhurried speaking voice.

Slightly thicker than convention. This is a deliberate encoding of a Layer 1 belief: *Craftsmanship as Respect*. The system's instinct is that readability is generous, not minimal. Text that is a touch heavier, a size step larger than expected, set with comfortable line height — this communicates that the designer thought about the person reading at the end. Not bold. Not loud. Just *present enough* that you never have to strain.

### Tonal Range

The Narrator is not a single setting. It has volume:

#### Header Narrator

The Narrator raising its voice. Bolder weight, larger size, used for section titles and structural landmarks. Still recognizably the same voice as body text — not a different instrument, just a different emphasis. Where the Declaration *commands*, the Header Narrator *organizes*.

#### Standard Narrator

The default reading voice. The workhorse. Optimized for sustained reading at body sizes. Regular or medium weight. Comfortable line height. The most-used setting in any text-heavy context.

#### Whisper Narrator

The Narrator at low volume. Lighter weight or reduced size. Captions, metadata, timestamps, graph labels, footnotes. The intimate end of the Monumental↔Intimate tension. It rewards the person who looks closer. It is still legible, still considered — not an afterthought in 9px light gray. The Whisper respects the reader just as much as the Standard; it simply speaks more quietly.

### The Narrator Sibling

Some contexts call for a different *feel* without a different *structure*. The Narrator Sibling is an alternate typeface that obeys the same rules — similar proportions, compatible metrics, equivalent tonal range — but brings a different personality.

The primary Narrator is the everyday voice. The Sibling is for contexts with more expressive freedom: a zine-style blog, a personal project, a layout where the typography carries more of the aesthetic weight. It is not used alongside the primary Narrator in the same piece. It is a *skin* — the same skeleton with different character.

**Selection principle:** The Sibling should feel like a different actor reading the same script. Different timbre, same pacing. If the primary Narrator is cast and the Sibling is placed next to it, you should think "these are related" not "these are the same" and not "these are strangers."
