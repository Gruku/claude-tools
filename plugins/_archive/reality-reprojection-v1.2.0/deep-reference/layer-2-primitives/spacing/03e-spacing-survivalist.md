## The Spatial Survivalist

On a 128×64 OLED, or any severely constrained display, the full spatial apparatus is unavailable. There is no room for generous margins. Grouping is limited. Depth layering may be impossible. The HUD may be reduced to nothing. What survives?

Two things carry the spatial system alone:

### Element Relations

Hierarchy through position and proportion. A large element above a small element establishes a clear primary/subordinate relationship. Elements aligned to a consistent edge establish structure. Even with minimal spacing available, the *relative* positions and sizes of elements communicate order. This is the spatial equivalent of the Technical voice carrying typography alone — reduced to essentials but still structured.

### Navigation Through the Lens

The principle that the viewport reveals a structure that extends beyond it. On a constrained display, the user navigates through content using physical controls (buttons, a dial, a touch gesture). The spatial structure exists beyond the visible frame, and movement through the viewport reveals it in sequence. The display is not showing "everything crammed into 128×64 pixels." It is showing a considered fragment of a larger structure — like a lens moving across a blueprint, revealing one section at a time.

### The Minimum Experience

The survivalist spatial experience should feel like looking through a small, well-framed window — not like staring at a cluttered postage stamp. Framing, even at its most minimal (a pixel or two of margin), is what creates this distinction. Composition, even at its most basic (one element above another with a clear gap between them), is what creates hierarchy.

The spatial survivalist is the system's proof that structure is not a luxury of large screens. It is a fundamental property that persists under constraint.
